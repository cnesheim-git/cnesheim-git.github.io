SEC Women’s Gymnastics Home Judging Bias is Overstated

gymnastics
ncaa
sec
Author

Cassie Nesheim

Published

April 10, 2026

Gymnastics fans have long speculated about “home bias” in judging. According to the gymternet (the mass of gymnastics enthusiasts on social media), judges are noticeably more lax and often award “gifts,” or scores that are higher than deserved, to the home team. For instance, a step on the landing in a routine is almost always an automatic 0.1 deduction. A routine with only one step and no other errors should score around a 9.85 or a 9.9, but at home, that routine might score a 9.95, which is an impossible score.

The question then becomes: do gymnasts actually score better when competing at home, and if so, which teams benefit most?

To understand whether bias could exist, it helps to start with the most dominant conference in college gymnastics, the SEC. Because of its position in the sport, this conference draws intense scrutiny, with fans regularly criticizing it for rampant home judging bias.

A look at the rankings confirms the SEC’s dominance. Rankings start at week 2 for college gymnastics, the first week of the season in which every team has competed. Since week 2, at least seven of the nine SEC teams have been in the top 10 in all of women’s college gymnastics, demonstrating their powerful command of the sport. If home judging bias exists, we would certainly expect it to show up in the SEC.

Home floor scores are almost 1.5 points higher than away floor scores. In gymnastics, this difference is huge. A standard statistical test confirmed that the floor scoring gap between home and away meets is not due to random chance. It is a real, meaningful difference. No other event showed significance, so the differences between home and away meets on the other events could be due to random chance. If the difference in home and away scoring were simply due to teams comfortable at home, this home scoring bump would show up significantly in the other events too.

Floor being the only significant event does make some sense in context. The home team typically competes floor last, and scores tend to loosen as competitions progress, conceivably benefiting the home team in the final event. So, higher scores are potentially awarded for routines that would have scored lower earlier in the competition.

Additionally, if bias is playing a role, judges may be trying to overcompensate in the last event to ensure the home team wins. Neither explanation proves intentional bias, but both offer plausible reasons why the large gap shows up on floor and nowhere else.

Floor tells part of the story, but the bigger picture lies in total team scores. If home judging bias exists, it should impact entire meets, not just one rotation. Do certain teams in the SEC experience more of a boost at home than others?

To answer that, team scoring averages at home and away were compared across the SEC. The gap between those two numbers reveals which programs gain the most from competing at home.

Top SEC teams receive noticeably higher scores at home than when away, whereas teams in the middle or bottom of the conference (with the exception of Auburn) see minimal differences.

Fans often criticize Oklahoma, LSU, and Florida for egregious home scoring, claiming those teams are being wildly overscored. It is important to keep in mind that these are also the top three teams in all of NCAA women’s gymnastics, so they have the most eyes on them. Also, those at the top often receive the most hate. Fans like the underdog and get frustrated when the top teams continue to perform well. So, while fans complain about these teams most, it doesn’t necessarily mean that their higher scores at home are actually due to a judging bias.

Using a standard statistical cutoff, where any number below -2 is considered significant, we can test teams to see if the difference between their home and away averages is due to random chance or could actually be representative of home judging bias. Though the data for Oklahoma, LSU, and Florida do demonstrate differences in average, when tested for significance, it is revealed that only one team in the SEC experiences a meaningful bump in scores at home, Oklahoma. Competing at home meaningfully inflates the Sooner’s scores, showing signs that they experience home judging bias.

For all other SEC teams, the differences in home and away scores exist but they are not significant, and we cannot say that home judging affects how well those teams do.

Home scores across the SEC are, on average, higher than those on the road, especially on floor. But when those differences are tested more closely and each team is examined, the story becomes less dramatic. Only Oklahoma shows a statistically meaningful home advantage, while the rest of the conference does not.

In a conference where dominance is the standard, home judging bias should be easy to detect. Instead, it largely fails to appear.

While fans may continue to debate home bias and argue the home team is being “gifted” scores, aside from the Sooners, their claims have no merit. If home judging bias were widespread, it would likely show up clearly in the top teams, like those in the SEC, but the data suggests otherwise. So, at least for now, gymnastics fans must come to terms with the fact that home scores are slightly higher, with the reassurance that it is not so sizeable that the results of each meet change depending on who is at home.

Home judging bias may exist in isolated cases, but it is far from having the dramatic effect it is often claimed to have on SEC teams.
