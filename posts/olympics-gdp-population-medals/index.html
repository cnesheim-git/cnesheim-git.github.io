Most everyone knows that the United States tends to dominate medal count at each Summer Olympics, usually placing within the top three countries, if not the very top. This includes leading in gold medals earned and overall total medals earned. China also consistently ranks among the top countries. What do these countries have in common? Massive GDP (gross domestic product). This means these countries have large economies, and, in theory, more resources to be able to compete well in Olympic games.

Looking at total medals compared to GDP, it is clear that the U.S. and China are in a league of their own. All the other countries end up in a small corner in the bottom left of the graph, signifying that the U.S. and China have such large GDPs and total medals earned that they overshadow the others. So, it is difficult to see how any other countries performed at the Paris 2024 Olympic Games compared to their GDP.

So, we have seen that GDP is an indicator of how well countries will do at the Games. While some countries are able to use their resources to their full potential, and even earn more medals than they are “supposed to”, others fail to rise to the occasion and perform worse than they should.

But what makes overperforming countries succeed? Do countries who overperform tend to rely on one or two disciplines (sports) to do so? Or do countries who underperform do so because they specialize in only one or two disciplines and fail to compete in other disciplines?

An analysis of which disciplines the overperforming countries medaled in revealed that, aside from Australia and Hungary, many countries used breadth to their advantage and medaled across many disciplines.

best_overperformers <- c("Australia", "France", "Great Britain", "Italy", "Japan", "New Zealand", "Hungary", "Uzbekistan")

best_events <- event_medals |>
  filter(country %in% best_overperformers) |>
  group_by(country, discipline) |>
  mutate(dis_count = n()) |>
  ungroup() |>
  group_by(country) |>
  mutate(discipline = ifelse(dis_count < max(dis_count)*0.10, "Other", discipline)) |>
  group_by(country, discipline) |>
  summarise(country_dis_count = sum(dis_count))

best_events <- best_events |>
  group_by(country) |>
  mutate(
    proportion = country_dis_count/sum(country_dis_count),
    country_max_prop = max(proportion)
  ) |>
  ungroup() |>
  mutate(
    percentage = paste0(round(proportion*100, 2), "%"),
    label = paste(discipline, percentage, sep = "\n"),
    country = reorder(country, -country_max_prop)
  )

While Australia relied on Swimming and Hungary relied on Canoe Sprint, all the other overperforming teams were able to do so by competing and medaling in a variety of disciplines. This breadth demonstrates how important it is for countries to be able to use their resources to diversify and provide ample opportunities to their people to train and compete in many, many disciplines. Now, does this principle hold for underperforming teams? In theory, teams who underperformed expectations did so because they were unable to take advantage of resources. If the idea that breadth is a key to success is true, then those teams who underperformed should lack diversity in the disciplines they medaled in. Let’s examine the breadth of the underperforming teams who took home at least two medals.

One thing to note, if a country only took home one medal, their breadth is obviously zero as their only medal could have only come from one discipline. So, these countries were excluded from breadth analysis.

Analyzing underperforming teams is tricky, because since they earned fewer medals than they were expected to, the number of medals is significantly smaller than those countries who overperformed. Though, underperforming countries can still be analyzed for their breadth. India relied heavily on Shooting and Mexico on Diving, though both countries medaled in at least four separate disciplines. Turkey medaled in five disciplines, but relied on Boxing pretty heavily. The United States, who took home the most medals in all of the games, relied heavily on Track and Field and Swimming, but also medaled in a variety of other disciplines. Lastly, Argentina was able to medal three times in three very separate disciplines, demonstrating impeccable breadth.

So, while underperforming countries did rely on certain disciplines more than overperforming countries did, the results are rather mixed and inconclusive. We can make some hypotheses on breadth, like more breadth is better, but overall there doesn’t appear to be a clear trend or pattern here.

While breadth may not be a good indicator of why certain countries exceeded expectations and others failed to reach their potential, using GDP is a rather accurate way to discover the countries who underperformed and overperformed at the Paris 2024 Olympic Games.

When all is said and done, GDP ends up accounting for about 75% of the variation in country medal earnings. This is pretty significant. This means that we can use a country’s GDP to predict how many medals they will earn at future Olympic Games reasonably well using just each country’s GDP. Now, as we saw, countries can underperform and overperform, so this prediction is not always accurate. However, we can definitely say that GDP has a relationship with how many medals each country earns.

We also saw an interesting pattern. In this analysis, some high-population countries tended to underperform. This was the case for the U.S., India, and Pakistan. All of these countries had the highest populations in their respective GDP groups and all emerged as one of the worst underperformers. So, while higher GDP indicates higher medal output, it seems that higher population may not have the same effect. This could be due to overcrowding and the need to allocate resources elsewhere, or it could be an extraneous factor. Either way, it is interesting that countries with higher populations ended up underperforming relative to their GDP expectations.

Overall, using GDP to discover which countries underperformed and overperformed at the Paris Games revealed India and the United States as the biggest losers, earning fewer medals than expected, and France and Great Britain as the biggest winners, earning significantly more medals than expected.
